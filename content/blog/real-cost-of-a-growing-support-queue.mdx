It's easy to measure a growing support queue by the obvious number: average
response time. But that's only the visible part of the cost. The fuller
accounting includes a few things that don't show up on a support dashboard.

**Agent burnout compounds.** A queue that never empties changes how agents work
— triage becomes reactive, breaks get skipped, and the tickets that need the
most care get the least, because there's no slack left to give them. Turnover
on support teams tracks queue pressure closely, and replacing an agent costs
far more than the salary line suggests once training time is counted.

**Escalations get noisier.** When agents are moving fast just to keep the queue
from growing, the tickets that genuinely need escalation — billing disputes,
angry customers, edge cases — get less scrutiny before they're passed up the
chain, which pushes the cost further downstream instead of solving it.

**CSAT erodes quietly.** Response time is only one input into satisfaction.
Reply quality drops under queue pressure too, and a fast-but-generic answer
often scores worse than a slightly slower, well-targeted one.

**The scaling math gets worse over time, not better.** Hiring to match ticket
growth works until it doesn't — support headcount growing in lockstep with
order volume is a cost structure that doesn't survive a downturn or a
seasonal spike.

The actual fix isn't answering faster with the same process. It's removing the
tickets that never needed a human in the first place, so the humans you do
have are working on the tickets that actually need them.
