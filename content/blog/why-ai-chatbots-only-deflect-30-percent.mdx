Most support teams that add an AI chatbot see the same number after a few months:
around 30% of tickets get deflected. The rest still land on a human agent — the
same overworked few people the chatbot was supposed to help.

This isn't a chatbot failure. It's a scope problem. Chatbots are built to have a
conversation with the *customer*. That's a narrow, high-stakes surface: get it
wrong and the customer notices immediately, so vendors tune chatbots to only
answer questions they're highly confident about — order status, shipping
policies, password resets. Everything with any ambiguity gets punted straight to
a human, unclassified and un-summarized.

The fix isn't a smarter chatbot. It's moving AI to the other side of the
conversation — the agent side — where a wrong guess doesn't reach the customer
before a human reviews it. That's the difference between "AI answers the
customer" and "AI clears the queue for the agent." The second one can safely
attempt far more than 30%, because every draft gets a human check before it
ships.

**What actually closes the gap:**

- **Classify everything, not just the easy stuff.** Every incoming ticket gets
  sorted into auto-resolve, draft-for-review, or escalate — nothing sits
  unclassified.
- **Draft, don't just detect.** A ticket routed to "draft-for-review" should
  arrive with an actual reply attached, grounded in your knowledge base, ready
  for an agent to approve in one click.
- **Escalate with context, not just a transfer.** When a ticket does need a
  human, it should arrive with a written summary — not just the raw thread the
  agent has to read cold.

Teams that restructure around this — AI drafts, human approves — routinely see
resolution rates well above the 30% chatbot ceiling, without changing anything
about what the customer experiences.
